Clause 4.1 of AS9100D is deceptively short. It asks your organisation to "determine external and internal issues that are relevant to its purpose and its strategic direction and that affect its ability to achieve the intended result(s) of its quality management system."

That's one sentence. It generates an enormous amount of confusion for small manufacturers because the standard doesn't tell you *how* to do it, what format the output should take, or how much analysis is enough.

Here's how I've approached it for a small UK aerospace manufacturer, and what I'd do differently with hindsight.

## What auditors are actually looking for

Before getting into methodology, it's worth being clear on what an AS9100D auditor expects to see from clause 4.1. They want evidence that your organisation has:

1. **Actively thought about** the context you operate in — not just filled in a template
2. **Connected that thinking** to your QMS scope and objectives
3. **Reviewed and updated** the analysis periodically, not just done it once for certification

The output format is genuinely flexible. A SWOT analysis, a PESTLE, a structured risk register, a narrative document — any of these works if it's clearly linked to your business and clearly tied into your planning processes (particularly clause 6.1, risks and opportunities).

What doesn't work: a generic downloaded template with placeholders half-filled in, or a document that could apply to any manufacturer anywhere.

## Internal issues — what to actually capture

For a small build-to-print shop, the internal issues that matter are usually:

**Capability and capacity**
- Which processes are genuinely under control, and which are held together by one person?
- Where are your single points of failure — in skills, equipment, or supplier relationships?
- What's your actual production capacity versus what you might be asked to quote for?

**Quality system maturity**
- How long has the QMS been operating in its current form?
- Are procedures followed because they're understood, or because someone checks?
- Where have you had escapes, concessions, or customer complaints in the last 12–24 months?

**People and culture**
- What's staff turnover doing to your tribal knowledge?
- Is quality seen as a function or a shared responsibility?
- Who in the business understands the AS9100D requirements well enough to sustain them?

**Infrastructure and technology**
- Is your production equipment calibrated, maintained, and adequate for the work you do?
- What are your IT dependencies — ERP, document control, drawing management?
- What happens if the person who runs the server goes off sick?

## External issues — the ones small manufacturers miss

External issues are often done superficially — a quick PESTLE that lists "economic conditions" and "regulatory requirements" without specificity. The ones that actually matter for a small aerospace manufacturer are more concrete:

**Customer concentration**
If 60% of your revenue comes from one prime contractor, that's a material external issue. Their scheduling behaviour, payment terms, drawing release practices, and strategic direction all directly affect your QMS's ability to deliver.

**Supply chain fragility**
Post-COVID, most small manufacturers have lived through material shortages, long-lead items, and sole-source supplier problems. These are external issues with direct QMS implications.

**Regulatory and standards environment**
AS9100D is the obvious one, but also: CAA Part 21/145 if you do MRO, ITAR/EAR if you work with US-origin technology, JOSCAR qualification requirements, and any customer-specific requirements (CSRs) from your prime contractors. Each of these is an external constraint on your QMS.

**Cyber security and data**
Increasingly relevant: the requirement for Cyber Essentials or DEFSTAN 05-138 compliance as a supply chain condition. If a prime contractor can delist you for failing a supplier audit that includes cyber checks, that's an external issue for clause 4.1.

**Market and competitive landscape**
Who else can do what you do? If you lose a key contract, what's the recovery path? This matters for understanding what your QMS needs to protect.

## Connecting 4.1 to the rest of the QMS

The point of clause 4.1 isn't the document itself — it's the thinking that feeds into:

- **Clause 4.4** (QMS scope): your context determines what's in and out of scope
- **Clause 6.1** (risks and opportunities): the issues you identify become inputs to your risk register
- **Clause 9.3** (management review): context should be revisited at each review

If your 4.1 analysis isn't visibly feeding into these other clauses, an auditor will notice. The standard is designed as a system, not a checklist.

## A practical format for small manufacturers

For a business under 50 people, I'd suggest a simple structured document covering:

1. **Who we are and what we do** — scope, products, processes, site(s)
2. **Internal issues** — organised by category (people, capability, infrastructure, QMS maturity), with a brief assessment of significance
3. **External issues** — organised by source (customers, regulators, supply chain, market, technology)
4. **How these connect to our QMS** — a short paragraph linking to scope, risks, and objectives
5. **Review history** — date last reviewed, what changed

Four to six pages is plenty for a small manufacturer. Auditors respond better to concise and specific than to long and generic.
