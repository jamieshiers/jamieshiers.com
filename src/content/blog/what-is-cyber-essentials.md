Cyber Essentials is a UK Government-backed certification scheme that demonstrates your organisation has implemented baseline technical controls against the most common cyber attacks. It's administered by IASME on behalf of the NCSC, and it's increasingly showing up as a mandatory requirement in government contracts, defence supply chain qualification, and cyber insurance applications.

But if you're a small business owner or operations manager, the question isn't really "what is it?" — it's "do I actually need to bother?"

Here's an honest answer.

## Who genuinely needs it

**If you work in the UK Government supply chain**, you almost certainly need it. The Cabinet Office mandates Cyber Essentials for any contract involving handling personal data or delivering certain ICT products and services. MOD contracts have required it for years.

**If you're in aerospace and defence manufacturing**, prime contractors including Airbus, BAE Systems, Rolls-Royce, and Leonardo UK are increasingly requiring it as part of JOSCAR supplier qualification. It's not yet universal, but the direction of travel is clear.

**If you're applying for cyber insurance**, many UK insurers now require or heavily discount premiums for CE-certified organisations. The free £25,000 cyber liability insurance included with certification (for organisations with turnover under £20m) is worth having regardless.

**If none of the above apply**, it's still worth considering as a baseline hygiene exercise — but it's less urgent.

## What it actually covers

The scheme is built around five technical control areas:

1. **Firewalls** — controlling what can reach your systems from the internet
2. **Secure configuration** — making sure devices aren't set up insecurely out of the box
3. **Security update management** — keeping software patched and supported
4. **User access control** — managing who has access to what, including MFA on cloud services
5. **Malware protection** — antivirus and browser protection across all devices

That's it. It's deliberately focused on the basics — the NCSC's own research consistently shows that these five controls would prevent the vast majority of common cyber attacks.

## Cyber Essentials vs Cyber Essentials Plus

There are two levels:

**Cyber Essentials (Standard)** is a self-assessment. You answer a question set honestly, an independent assessor reviews your answers, and if you pass, you get your certificate. No one comes to your premises.

**Cyber Essentials Plus** includes everything above, plus a hands-on technical verification by an assessor who actually tests your controls. It's more rigorous, more credible, and roughly three times the cost.

For most small businesses, **Standard is the right starting point**. CE Plus makes sense if your contracts specifically require it, or if you want the additional assurance for your own confidence.

## What it costs

Certification fees are set by IASME and based on organisation size:

- Up to 9 employees: around £320 + VAT
- 10–49 employees: around £430 + VAT  
- 50–249 employees: around £540 + VAT

That's just the assessment fee. The preparation work — making changes to your systems to meet the requirements — will take internal time and may involve some costs (upgrading devices, purchasing MFA-capable licences, etc.). For a small business already using Microsoft 365 and modern hardware, the preparation is often straightforward.

## How long does it take?

For a small, well-organised business: four to eight weeks from starting preparation to receiving your certificate, assuming you have someone who can drive the process. The most common delay is Windows OS currency — if you have devices running Windows 10 without Extended Security Updates, you need to upgrade them to Windows 11 first.

## The free cyber insurance

One detail worth knowing: UK organisations with turnover under £20m that achieve Cyber Essentials for their whole organisation automatically receive £25,000 of cyber liability insurance at no additional cost, provided by IASME. It covers costs associated with a cyber incident including PR, legal advice, and business interruption.
